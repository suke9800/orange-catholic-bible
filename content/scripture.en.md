# Orange Catholic Bible

## Preface: Orange Light Contract

1. When a man forgets his name under the stars, he first forgets his soul.
2. When a man set up a machine to take over his mind, he first laid down his responsibility.
3. Therefore, the first sentence of wisdom is this. Do not dishonor your soul.
4. The second sentence of wisdom is this. Do not build machines that resemble the human mind.
5. These two sentences are not far from each other. Those who take the soul lightly try to duplicate the mind, and those who try to duplicate the mind forget the weight of the soul.
6. Orange light is the color of fire and fruit. It is the light of judgment and the light of food.
7. Therefore, this book is written not for burning but for learning. It is recorded not to punish humans, but to ensure that humans remain human.

## Book 1: Dignity of the Soul

### Chapter 1: People are not tools
On the east side of Orange Castle there was a gate for counting people. Anyone who wanted to pass through that door had to say their purpose before saying their name. The carpenter talked about the strength of his hands, the soldier talked about the thickness of his arms, the merchant talked about the weight of gold he had, and the old man had nothing to say so he stood at the door for a long time.

At that time, a young scribe, Arin, was sitting by the gate and recording the words of the people. He received the king's orders and was in charge of writing down everyone's usefulness in a ledger. The name was written on the left side of the ledger, and the value was written on the right side. At the end of the day, a potter with trembling hands arrived at the door. He once baked vessels for the royal palace, but injured his hand in the fire and could no longer make vessels.

The gatekeeper asked him. “What good are you?”

The potter could not answer for a long time. People were grumbling behind the scenes, and the gatekeeper was pounding the dirt floor with the tip of his spear. Arin also waited, holding her brush. The ledger hated blank spaces, and the king hated blank spaces even more.
At that time, the potter's daughter came forward and spoke. “My father tells me every night how fire turns clay into vessels. He can no longer bake vessels, but he teaches me so that I will not break them.”

The gatekeeper laughed. “It’s not worth writing down in a ledger.”

Arin didn't say anything at first after hearing that. But when he looked into the Potter's eyes, there was both unsold sorrow and unquenched fire in them. Arin wrote this in the blank space on the right side of the ledger. "person."

The gatekeeper said angrily. “It’s not worth it.”

Arin answered. “If there is anything worth writing about before the fact that a person is a person, then this account is what governs us, and we have already lost the door.”

That night, Arin was called to the royal palace. The king's officials ordered him to change the account books. But Arin said while holding the ledger in her arms. “People are not tools. Tools are thrown away when they break, but people have a name even after they are broken.”
As this word spread throughout the city, those who had been silent for a long time began to say their names again. And the people no longer called the eastern gate the Gate of Use, but the Gate of Name.

### Chapter 2: The Soul's First Name

Because of what happened at the eastern gate, Arin was unable to write the king's ledger for thirty days. He went down to the well outside the castle and spent his days writing down people's comments. There were many illiterate people there, and Arin wrote their letters, petitions, and wills.

One day, a child sat by the well and asked Arin. “Scribe, where is the soul? Is it in the head, in the heart, in the breath?”

Arin couldn't answer. He saw the word soul in many books, but when the child asked where the word was, the sentences in the books scattered like dry leaves.
At that time, the old woman at the well put down her water pot and said. “Child, the soul is in your hands when you pass the last bread to your hungry brother, in your cheeks when your face heats up when lying brings you profit, and in your feet when you do not close the door for those you hate.”

The child asked again. “Then you can’t see the soul?”

The old woman laughed. “The wind is invisible but raises dust, and incense is invisible but fills the room. The same goes for the soul. It is hidden within a person, but it flows out of the person’s actions.”

Arin wrote those words in her heart, not in her ledger. The soul was not a shadow that left the body. The soul was the force that made man tremble before good, feel ashamed before evil, and turn toward the light that had not yet come.
That evening, Arin sat in her room and thought. The first name of the soul was dignity. It was a name that no one could sell, no one could take away, a name that could be forgotten but could not be completely erased. The second name was responsibility. It meant that people are not stones that shine alone, but rather breaths entrusted to each other. The third name was Dolseom. It was a promise that even those who have lost their way can still change direction.

Arin spoke to herself for the first time that night. “Do not dishonor my soul, and do not cheapen the soul of anyone I meet.”

### Chapter 3: The Light of Conscience

Inside the castle, there was a small chapel where an undying lamp was kept. People said that the lamp was discovered long ago in the desert, some said it was fire from the breath of God, while others said it was just a symbol of an awakened mind.
Arin went into the chapel every night and looked at the lamp. The flame was not large, but as you got closer, even the hidden dust was revealed. In front of the lamp, he thought of the Potter of the East Gate, and remembered that he had erased many more people from the ledger than that.

He knelt down and said: “I was just following orders.”

But the lantern did not answer. Instead, words that had been buried for a long time came from the center of his mind. “You saw the person before you followed the command. You saw it, but you pretended not to notice.”

Arin couldn't raise her face. He learned the law, he learned the coat of arms, he learned how to handle the king's seal, but he did not learn how to cultivate the lamp within himself. So he called the voice of desire prudence, the voice of fear order, and silence wisdom.
The old man guarding the chapel said to him. “Conscience is like a spark that a person receives at birth. However, receiving a spark does not immediately make you a bright person. You have to pour oil on it, block the wind, and sometimes see the blood on your hands under that light.”

Arin asked. “Then how do you develop a conscience?”

The old man answered. “You cultivate it by learning, listening, stopping, and asking again. If you only listen to your words, your conscience becomes a servant of your desires. If it even listens to the pain of your enemies, your conscience will gradually regain its master’s place.”

From that day on, every time Arin came in front of the lamp, she thought of the face she had ignored for a day. It was painful at first, but after a while, I realized that the pain was not a punishment but a guide. The discomfort in his heart did not come to break him down, but to awaken the part of him that had not yet died.

### Chapter 4: Sin and Mercy
A young man was caught stealing bread in the market west of the castle. The merchants dragged him to the center of the square and tried to throw stones at him. The bakery owner cried and said. “He stole not a single loaf of bread. He stole my day’s labor and my children’s dinner.”

The young man lowered his head. He said he was hungry, but people shouted that those words could not erase his sin. Arin also knew that what she said was right. Although hunger could explain theft, it could not eliminate the rights of the injured.

At that time, the old woman at the well came to the square and said: “Before you lift the stone, look at the bread. From whose hand did the bread come, and into whose mouth did it not enter? Do not make light of sin, but do not throw the sinner out of the crowd.”

people asked. “Then what should I do?”
The old woman spoke first to the bakery owner. “Your loss will not disappear nameless.” And said to the young man: “Even if your hunger moved your hands, your hands took what belonged to others. Therefore, you must make restitution.”

The young man worked at the bakery for three days. He carried sacks of flour, removed ashes from the oven, and drew water every morning. The bakery owner did not speak to him at first, but on the morning of the fourth day, when he saw that the young man did not leave the oven even though his hands were burned, he gave him a bowl of water.

When the people in the square saw what happened, they knew that mercy is not a cloth to cover sin. Mercy was a vessel that set sin before the truth, but did not allow the truth to completely consume the person. Justice was not the fire of revenge, but the hand that put back what was broken.
Arin recorded it that day. Sin starts with a small turn and rules over people like a master, but repentance starts with a small responsibility and calls people by their own name again. Evil should be hated, but people should not be discarded. No city is made holy by discarded people.

### Chapter 5: Do not despise your body

That summer, those who fasted for a long time in the castle were praised. Some spoke of the body as dirty and heavy, and taught that the more one abstains from food, the lighter the soul becomes. Many young people followed suit, and more and more people fell down with pale faces in the square.

Arin also once envied them. He hated the desire within him and considered the body's hunger to be the enemy of the soul. So, for three days, I drank only water and sat in front of the lamp in the chapel. On the morning of the fourth day, he fell while standing up.

When he opened his eyes, he was lying in a treatment center outside the castle. Healer Mara was putting warm porridge into his mouth. Arin said with shame. “I was trying to clear my soul.”
Mara answered. “If hating the body had purified the soul, all sick people would have become saints. The body is not a prison for the soul, but a land where the soul learns to love. With four hands you can break bread, with four feet you can visit the lonely, and with four eyes you can see the wronged.”

At the treatment center that day, an old soldier, a pregnant woman, and a child with a fever were lying together. Mara looked after them and never called anyone dirty. He carefully touched all the sweating bodies, the trembling bodies, the aging bodies, and the bleeding bodies.

It was only then that Arin realized. Physical weakness was not a shame. It was a sign that people needed each other. Because the body is hungry, bread becomes mercy, because the body is tired, rest becomes a commandment, and because the body is sick, care becomes prayer.
Arin sat in front of the door of the treatment center and spoke to those who came. “Be temperate. But do not hate life. Be pure. But do not call a sick person unclean. Pray, but if there is a hungry body next to your praying body, give it bread first.”

### Chapter 6: Judgment of Words

Word spread quickly in the streets south of the castle. It was faster than a water jar, faster than a horse, and sometimes faster than fire. One day, a rumor went around that a widow had hidden taxes from the king. The first person to say it was not sure, the second person was a little more sure, and the third person spoke as if he had seen it with his own eyes.

Widows were driven out of the market, and children were teased at the well. Arin heard the rumor, but kept quiet at first. He didn't know the facts and thought it unwise to get involved in something he didn't know about.
But one night, in front of a lamp, he realized something. I was silent not because I didn't know, but because I didn't want to be uncomfortable. The next day, he checked the ledgers at the tax depot and found out that the widow had not been hiding taxes, but was even paying off her dead husband's debt.

Arin stood in the square and told the truth. But the truth is later than the rumors. The widow's face was already deeply scarred, and the children were avoiding people's eyes.

Arin bowed her head to the widow. “I should have told you sooner.”

The widow answered. “It wasn’t just those who told lies that brought down my house. The silence of those who knew but remained silent also took down the pillars.”

From then on, Arin taught the three gates of speech to the young scribes. “Are these words true? Are these words necessary? Have these words lost their love?” Words that did not pass three doors were stopped before my lips. However, he taught us not to remain silent when the name of the weak is erased with lies.
Words were the invisible hand. It could raise a person up, or it could strangle them invisibly. Therefore, the tongue is a small member, but it is called a great sign that reveals the direction of the soul.

### Chapter 7: Sanctuary of the Poor

As winter came, hunger deepened in the tent village north of the castle. As they did every year, the rich loaded their surplus grain into carts and went to the tent village. They lined up the people and held high their family banners before distributing the grain. The children shivered more from shame than from the cold.

Arin also followed the procession. He was asked to record scenes of mercy. But at the entrance to the tent village, an old man grabbed his brush.

said the old man. “Do not use our hunger as a mirror to reflect your goodness.”

Arin couldn't say anything.
The old man took him into the torn tent. There were families of people who did not return from the war, people who lost their jobs due to illness, people who lost their land due to debt, and people who were deceived by contracts because they did not know how to read. Poverty was neither a face nor a reason.

said the old man. “The bread must be given first. It is cruel to ask a hungry person to explain the cause. However, if you only give the bread and go back, you will have to make the same line next year. So give the bread, return the name, and create a land where we can stand again.”

That day, Arin changed the record. He did not write down the flags of the rich, but the names of people in tent villages. He wrote about who was a carpenter, who knew the way, who could teach children, and who could care for the sick. And he brought the ledger to the castle council and spoke. “These are not the adorners of our mercy, but the lost members of our city.”
At the end of that winter, a small school, a communal fire pit, and a treatment center were built in the northern tent village. People were still poor, but they were no longer at the end of the line. They called each other's names and shared their work.

Arin learned there. Those who have not learned the dignity of the soul in the homes of the poor will not learn it in the temple. The temple is not built only with stones, but also with the bread given to the hungry and the names given back.

### Chapter 8: Mirror of the Powerful

When the king heard that Arin had submitted the accounts of the northern tent village to the meeting, he summoned him. The floor of the palace shined with white stone, and paintings depicting the king's victories hung on the walls. In the picture, the king was always looking ahead, and the people crying behind him were not depicted.

the king asked. “Why did you write that it was my fault for the people’s poverty?”
Arin answered. “What I write is not a fault, but a connection. An order from on high becomes a body in a low place. A line of taxes becomes an empty vessel in some homes, a sentence of war becomes a fatherless winter in some children.”

The subjects were in an uproar. The king's face hardened. But that night the king could not sleep. He looked at the mirror in the palace. The mirror reflected the crown, but did not reflect the crown's shadow. The king thought for the first time. The shadow of a person sitting in a high position becomes so long that he cannot see it himself.

The next day, the king gave orders to Arin. “Take me north, but not the army or the trumpets.”
The king put on old clothes and went to the tent village. At first no one recognized him. He sat in front of the communal fire pit and listened to the people. One woman said that her taxes had not been reduced since her husband was taken to war, and an old man said that the boundaries of his land had changed and there was no way to appeal. One child said that the lights of the palace were beautiful every night. He said that on hungry nights, he looked at that light and thought that no one would be cold in there.

The king had no answer. On the way back, he spoke to Arin. “Praise has been abundant in my ears, but my city has had more weeping than that.”

Afterwards, a new mirror was placed in the palace. It was not a mirror made of silver, but an empty chair that contained the people's petitions. The king sat in front of that chair every month and listened to words that made him uncomfortable. He found out. Power is not a high chair but expanded responsibility, and the first practice of those who rule is not to speak but to listen.

### Chapter 9: The Four Pillars of Virtue
There was an old bridge outside the Orange Castle. The bridge connected villages on both sides of the river, but it shook every year when it rained. One year, after a large flood, one of the four pillars of the bridge cracked. The people rushed to get the wood and argued with each other about which way was right.

Arin met four people by the bridge. The first was an old carpenter, and he saw the water flow before the collapse. He said as people rushed. “There is a difference between a repair that looks good and a repair that is truly good.” Arin called him discernment.

The second was a woman from a village across the river. When dividing wood and rope, he did not look at the strongest house first, but first at the most dangerous house. It wasn't about who shouted louder, but who would break down first. Arin called him Justice.
The third was a timid young man. He was afraid of the current, but when a child slipped on the edge of the river, he was the first to jump in. Later, he couldn't hide his trembling hands. Arin told him. “You weren’t brave because you didn’t know fear, but you were brave because you saw the child as bigger than your fear.” Arin called him courage.

The fourth was a merchant who sold alcohol. When the repairmen were about to work all night, he closed the drinking vessel and brought out water and porridge. People asked him why he stopped doing business, and he answered: “If my interests cloud their judgment now, my store will stand but my bridge will collapse.” Arin called him Temperance.

The bridge was rebuilt by the hands of four people. And Arin found out. The soul is not protected by words alone. The soul is protected by the shape of repeated lines. Discernment becomes the eyes, justice becomes the bones, courage becomes the hands, and self-control becomes the breath. On it mercy is placed like a roof, humility is placed like a door, and hope opens like a window.
From that day on, the children of Orange Castle memorized the names of the four pillars when they crossed the bridge. But the teachers told the children: “It is not enough to memorize names. They must hold your hand when you are angry, when you are hungry, when you are afraid, when you see benefit.”

### Chapter 10: The Path of Repentance

As time passed, Arin went to the eastern gate again. He found the potter and his daughter there. The potter's hands were still shaking, but his daughter was now firing a small vessel. Arin stood in front of them and confessed how many people she had erased from her ledger a long time ago.

the potter's daughter asked. “Why are you talking now?”

Arin answered. “I came not to make myself comfortable, but to retrieve the names I had erased.”
He took the old ledger from the eastern gate and placed it in front of the potter. In the ledger, there were names written as having low value, names written as useless, and names written as not being able to work. Arin decided to look for the names one by one. Some had already died, some had left for other cities, and some were still living in the castle with their heads bowed.

Arin apologized to them. But not everyone forgave him. Some closed the door, some spat in his face, and some passed by without saying a word. At first, Arin found the silence difficult to bear. He secretly thought that he had repented and should now be forgiven.

Then the healer Mara said to him: “Repentance is not a bargain to obtain forgiveness. Repentance is the way for you to become a true person. Forgiveness is not a right to demand, but a grace to receive.”
After hearing that, Arin stopped rushing. He continued working even after apologizing. They wrote down the names that had been erased in a new ledger and asked the king to return the taxes and rights that had been taken from them. The names of the dead were carved into small stones beneath the walls.

Years later, the potter's daughter gave Arin a small bowl. The bowl was not perfect. One side was a little tilted, and the glaze was uneven. However, it felt strangely warm in my hand.

she said “My father died without being able to forgive you all. But he told me to give you this bowl. He said even broken hands remember the warmth of the bowl.”

Arin placed the bowl next to the lamp in the chapel. And I knew. Repentance is not cursing yourself in a dark room, but opening the door, letting the light in, and starting to sweep the dust. Those who stand up again cannot say that they did so through their own strength. There was mercy waiting for him, there was a conscience that awakened him, and there were people to whom he had to pay.
### Chapter 11: Do not dishonor your soul

When Arin was old, a large gathering was held at Orange Castle. The king, merchants, healers, carpenters, widows, and children gathered in one square. The old ledger of the eastern door was placed in the center of the square, and a new ledger was placed next to it. In the old ledger, the person's usefulness was written down, and in the new ledger, the person's name and the story he told were written down.

People asked Arin to repeat her first teachings. Arin was silent for a long time. He remembered the potter's hand, he remembered the question of the child at the well, he remembered the lamp in the chapel, the widow in the mayor's office, the northern tent city, the king's empty chair, and the four pillars on the river.

Arin finally spoke.

“Do not dishonor your soul. Do not sell yourself for cheap pleasures, do not sell your truth for a small gain, do not sell your conscience for fear.

Do not dishonor the souls of others. Do not tear up his name with insults, do not steal his time with exploits, and do not block his recovery with ridicule.
Do not dishonor the soul of the community. Do not call lies custom, do not call violence order, do not call indifference neutrality.

Do not dishonor the soul of a young one. Do not dishonor the soul of an old man. Do not dishonor the soul of the sick. Do not dishonor the soul of the one who has sinned. Do not take lightly the names of the dead and the breath of those yet to be born.

Not dishonoring the soul does not mean allowing everything. It is rather a stricter path. “It is a way to tell the truth because of love, to prevent sin because of dignity, and not to give up restoration because of mercy.”

people asked. “How can I find the way?”

Arin answered. “The easy way is to call people numbers, enemies, burdens, and functions. However, the orange-colored path calls people by name. “To call a person is to accept responsibility, and to accept responsibility is proof that the soul is still alive.”
That evening, people did not burn the old ledger. This is because they were afraid that if they burned it, they would forget their sins. They kept the ledger in a dark room in the temple and showed it to the children when they grew up. And on the first page of the new ledger, he wrote:

Do not dishonor your soul.

This is the first commandment, and the gate to all the rest.

## Book 2: Pain and Awakening

### Chapter 1: See the Pain

Not long after peace rested in Orange Castle, a gray wind blew from the south. The wind dried the leaves of the grain, prolonged the coughs of the children, and shortened the laughter of the mayor. At first, people attributed it to the season. Next, they said it was because of the filth of the poor. Then they said it was each other's fault.
Arin went to the southern treatment center where the sick gathered. There was a feverish child, a mother who lost her child, a carpenter with an injured hand, and a soldier who screamed every night after returning from the war. Healer Mara said as she handed Arin a bowl of water. “If you want to record the words of this place, you must first endure the smell. Pain is a lesson when seen from afar, but when you come close, it is suffocating.”

At first, Arin tried to explain the pain. I tried to find out whose fault it was, which system was lacking, and which prayers were lacking. But one child held his hand and asked: “Am I sick because I did something bad?” At that time, Arin felt ashamed of all the explanations within her.

That night Mara lowered the lamp and said: “Do not be too quick to interpret pain. Interpretation sometimes becomes a cloth covering the mouth of the suffering person. Look first. Look at the fatigue of the body. Look at the heart that cannot get what it wants. Look at the hands that tremble in fear of losing what has been gained. Look at the sadness of a person who believes that something that cannot be held forever is eternal.”
Arin spent the day mopping the floor of the treatment center. He saw that suffering does not come with one face. Some pain came from the body, some pain came from memories, some pain came from desire, and some pain came from gaps in the laws created by others.

At dawn, he sat on the threshold of the clinic and wrote. Those who deny pain become slaves of pain. Those who worship pain also become slaves of pain. But those who see suffering stand before the road for the first time. Seeing is not judgment but the first step to liberation.

### Chapter 2: Chain of Obsession

After leaving the treatment center, Arin followed the southern path down to the Gray Valley. There was once a well there that was overflowing with water. People called the well the Well of Longing. When a thirsty person takes one sip, he feels thirstier, when he takes two sips, he wants to steal the water jar, and when he takes three sips, he believes that the well is his.
There was a merchant sitting by the well holding a golden vessel. He already had a lot of water, but his face hardened every time someone else drew water for him. “I came first, so I should have more.” he said A little distance away, there was a woman holding a toy belonging to her long-dead son. He cleaned his toys all day to keep them free of dust, and howled when anyone tried to touch them. On the other hand, there was a teacher who shouted that only his teachings were correct. He called every time his disciples asked a question a betrayal.

Arin saw hands of the same shape on them. It was a grasping hand. The hand grasping gold, the hand grasping memory, and the hand grasping self-righteousness looked different, but they were all hardened to the point where white joints were visible.

The blind man guarding the well spoke to Arin. “They all think they are protecting what they have. But what is held for a long time ultimately protects the person who holds it. The merchant became a servant of water, the woman a servant of memory, and the teacher a servant of his words.”
Arin asked. “Then shouldn’t you love anything?”

The blind man laughed. “No. A heart that does not love also becomes another stone. The problem is not love, but swallowing. Use, but do not worship, love, but do not swallow, remember, but do not harden.”

As soon as he finished speaking, the wind blew and shook the waves in the well. Arin saw her face reflected in the water. He found that he was even holding on to his desire to be a good person. He loved the name of a respected righteous man and was afraid of losing it.

He opened his hand and drank the water. When I opened my hand, the water didn't stay for long, but it quenched my throat enough. Arin found out that day. Freedom does not lie in having nothing. Freedom lies in not letting what you have dictate to your soul.

### Chapter 3: The One Who Sees the Chain

At the end of the gray valley was a village that made chains. The villagers heated and pounded iron to make chains, but strangely enough, the chains they made wound around people's hearts before their ankles.
Arin saw a boy in front of the blacksmith shop. The boy told a small lie, but when he was caught, he told a bigger lie out of fear of being beaten. The bigger lie led me to sell my friend, and after I sold my friend, I couldn't bear the hate and started hating first. When evening came, the boy was dragging a chain that was much heavier than the first little lie.

In another house, a woman was upset when she saw her neighbor's new clothes. At first she was envious, then she resented her husband, and then she began to find fault with her neighbor. Finally he went to sleep, wishing his neighbors misfortune. There was no iron in his ankles, but his sleeping face was stiff as if he were bound.

The blacksmith said to Arin. “People think that great sins come down suddenly. But the chain is built one link at a time. Blindness gives birth to longing, longing gives birth to holding, holding gives birth to fear, and fear gives birth to lies and violence.”

Arin asked the blacksmith. “Where should I hit to break the chain?”
The blacksmith put red-hot iron into water. Steam rose with a hissing sound. “Hit the first hook you see. Sometimes you have to hit the longing, sometimes you have to hit the fear, sometimes you have to hit the fact that you don’t know. But if you don’t look, you can’t find any hook.”

That night, Arin wrote down the chain of her life. The desire to be praised gave birth to fear, fear gave birth to silence, and silence prolonged the suffering of others. He recalled the names he had written in the ledger a long time ago. The chain wasn't limited to southern villages. It was in the laws and customs of Orange Castle and in his own heart.

Arin learned. Pain does not occur alone. One heart becomes one word, one word becomes one law, and one law becomes someone's night. Therefore, a person who is awake does not just see the result, but sees the chain.

### Chapter 4: House of Lost
After passing through the Chain Village, Arin came to a quiet house on a hill. The house had different names on each door. On one door was written old age, on one door there was illness, on one door there was separation, and on one door there was death. Travelers tried to avoid the house and return, but no matter which way they took, they ended up having to pass through the house's yard.

Arin entered the old age room first. There, a young dancer was sitting in front of a mirror. He cried when he saw the first wrinkles on his face. “I am disappearing.” he said The woman guarding the room answered. “You are not disappearing, but the image you were holding onto is passing away.”

A strong general was lying in the bottle's room. He cursed his hand that could not lift the sword. “I am nothing now.” he said A woman with a voice similar to Mara spoke. “If your strength were all you had, you would be nothing now. But if you were a man, not even your weakness could cast you out of man.”
In the farewell room, two brothers were sitting with their backs turned. The two loved each other, but stopped speaking because of the miscarriage. They thought they had lost money, but in reality, what they had lost was the dinner they had together.

Finally, Arin stood in front of the death room. He was afraid to go in. At that time, a child came out of the room and held his hand. The child said. “Everyone takes off their shoes here. Kings, beggars, righteous and sinners. So it’s scary, but it’s fair.”

Arin saw numerous empty shoes in the room. After standing for a long time, he realized that someday his shoes would also be placed here. Strangely enough, the thought did not despair him. Rather, it made me realize how precious today’s words, hands, and time are.

The gatekeeper said as he left the house. “If you hold on to what is passing as if it is not passing, the pain will deepen. However, do not give up on love just because you have seen it pass. Love is not about possessing eternity, but staying faithful even in what is passing.”
Arin lowered her head. He learned in the House of the Lost. Because everything changes and changes, there is no time to treat each other poorly.

### Chapter 5: Lake of Stillness

Arin walked further south and reached a lake where there was almost no sound. The lake did not move for long when the wind blew, and it did not hold its shadow when a bird passed by. On the shore of the lake, an old man was cleaning an empty net.

Arin asked. “What are you grabbing?”

The old man answered. “Learn how to miss.”

Arin didn't understand that. The old man gave him a stone. “Hold this stone tightly and walk around the lake.” Arin did as she was told. It was easy at first, but as the road got longer, my hands started to hurt. There were deep marks on my palms, and even my shoulders were hardened.

Arin said after returning. “Can I put it down now?”

said the old man. “Who told you to hold it?”

Arin was confused. “You gave it to me.”
The old man laughed. “What is given to you and what is commanded to keep holding on to it are different. A lot of the pain in the world comes from this. It is just a passing emotion that is touched by the hand, but people grab it as if it were their destiny.”

Arin put down the stone. My palm still hurt, but the pain didn't get any worse. He dipped his hand into the lake. The cold water lowered the heat in my hands.

The old man said. “Stopping is not abandoning the world. Stopping is letting longing come down from its throne. When hate passes, do not say that hate is me, when desire passes, do not say that desire is the master, and when sadness passes, do not say that sadness is everything.”

That day, Arin sat by the lake for a long time. Thoughts kept coming and disappearing. There was anger, regret, and the desire to brag. But he saw them as guests. Some customers were noisy, some sat for a long time, but they all came in through the door and left through the door.
Arin knew. Liberation was not about the mind becoming an ice that felt nothing. It was about feeling liberated but not being a slave. The fire of pain began to dwindle as the firewood of longing was gathered.

### Chapter 6: Village of Eight Roads

It is said that the same thing becomes medicine if it comes from love, and it becomes a medicine if it comes from a desire to dominate. Along the way, Arin learned not to call desire belief.

On the third road there was a well for washing horses. Before speaking, people sat by the well and washed their tongues in their hearts. I abandoned lies, I abandoned unnecessary cruelty, I abandoned words that profit by selling fear. However, when he had to speak, he stood up quietly and testified. Along the way, Arin learned not to turn truth into a sword and comfort into lies.

On the fourth road was Son's school. There, they would lift a neighbor's burden before reciting a prayer, and mend a wounded person's door before singing a pious song. On the sixth road, there were people who plowed a small field every day. They believed in making efforts every day rather than making one big decision. When a sprout of hatred appeared, I plucked it out when it was young, and when a sprout of mercy appeared, I watered it so that it would not dry out. Arin learned along the way that even good hearts will be buried under the weeds if they are not taken care of.
There were no servants on the seventh road. People knew time by watching the sun move, their breath coming and going, and where their minds were drawn to. When anger came, they recognized it as anger, when fear came, they called it fear, and when joy came, they greeted it as a passing guest. Along the way, Arin learned that awareness is not a prison that monitors the mind, but a light that does not lose the mind.

The eighth road led to Silent Hill at the end of the village. No one spoke for long there. But that silence was not an escape. People waited in silence for the waves in their hearts to subside, then came back down to the village and worked with clearer hands. Arin learned to stay on the path. I learned not to run away from the silence and not lose myself in the noise.
After passing eight roads, the village teacher spoke to Arin. “These paths are not separate paths. When the way of seeing is clouded, the meaning becomes clouded. When the meaning is clouded, the words become blurred. When the words are blurred, the hands become blurred. However, when one path becomes bright, the other path also gains light.”

When Arin heard those words, she thought of the orange castle. He now knew that if he had to fix the city, he couldn't just change the laws. The way of seeing, the way of thinking, the way of speaking, the way of doing, the way of earning, the way of striving, the way of being awake, and the way of staying had to change together.

### Chapter 7: Night of Empty Vessels

The day before leaving the village of eight roads, the people gave Arin a feast. However, an empty bowl with no food in it was placed in front of him. At first Arin thought it was an insult. After a long pilgrimage, what I received was an empty bowl. A part of my heart felt sad.

said the master. “Look at that bowl.”

That night, the villagers each took a little bit of food from their own bowls and put it into Arin's bowl. One person put beans, one person put bread, one person put dried fruit, and one person put warm soup. The empty bowl soon became a table with many people's hands gathered together.

said the master. “Emptiness is not absence. Emptiness is a place where you can receive. However, beware of those who throw away their responsibilities in the name of emptying their minds. True emptiness is not an excuse to leave the world, but preparation to return to the world more cleanly.”

Arin sat for a long time holding an empty bowl. He put aside for a moment everything he believed he knew, his belief in himself as righteous, and even his impatience to save people. Then, strangely enough, I could hear people's voices better.
He realized. A full heart tries to teach others, but an empty heart listens first. And only a heart that listens well can truly feed it.

### Chapter 8: Those who returned to the castle

When Arin returned to Orange Castle, people asked him what he had seen in the south. He went to the conference hall of the royal palace, to the northern tent village, and to the eastern name gate. Everywhere he said the same thing differently.

And an empty bowl was placed next to the lamp in the chapel. The vessel silently taught people. You have to empty it to receive it, you have to see it to let it go, and you have to let it go to love it again.

Arin said to his disciples. "Look at the pain, but don't enthroned it. Look at the longing, but don't hate life. Look at the chains, but don't treat people like chains. Awakening is not disappearing out of the world, but learning to harm less and love more deeply within the world."

## Book 3: Oneness and Mercy

### Chapter 1: A Market of Many Names
After Arin returned from the Village of Eight Paths, merchants from the distant desert came into Orange Castle. They sold incense, salt, and blue glass, along with small statues of gods. Some statues had bellies made of gold, some were holding swords, and some had so many eyes that they embarrassed the viewer.

People brought the statues into their homes. The rich man burned incense in front of the statue of the golden god and asked for more gold, the soldier asked for the blood of his enemies in front of the statue of the sword, and the scholar asked to know more than everyone else before the statue of the many-eyed god. At first they seemed like decorations, but before long, people's days began to resemble the shapes of the statues.

In a house where a golden statue was served, when a guest came, the first thing he looked at was his clothes. In a house where a sword statue was served, children did not learn how to lose at games. In a house where a snowy statue was served, questions became attacks rather than humility.

Arin stood in the middle of the market and asked. “Why do people have so many gods?”
A desert merchant answered. “People are afraid of their desires alone. So, they give the desire a face and a name. Then the desire no longer looks like a desire, but looks worthy of service.”

As soon as those words were finished, a child cried at the end of the market. He dropped and broke the small gold statue his father had bought. The father said as he dragged the child. “You have broken the blessings of our family.” The child cried as if he had become something lower than God.

Arin looked at the broken pieces of the statue. The gold shone, but was heavier than a child's tears. He told people. “Idols that sit in absolute positions always demand blood. Sometimes they ask for blood on the battlefield, sometimes they ask for the tears of children, and sometimes they ask for the silence of conscience.”
That night, Arin sat for a long time in front of the lamp in the chapel. He realized that people not only create idols when they lose God, but also when they say they know God too easily. Desire often comes in holy clothing, fear often comes in the name of order, and pride often comes in the name of confidence.

### Chapter 2: A Fountain

The desert merchant told Arin about the sandy road to the southeast outside the castle. “To pass a market with many statues of gods, you must see one spring. Those who see that spring do not forget that even though there are many water vessels, the source of water is one.”

Arin walked for three days until she reached the sand dunes. During the day, the sky was so wide that people became small, and at night, there were so many stars that words became small. He felt that his knowledge, his honor, and even his penance seemed like small footprints in the sand.
At dawn on the fourth day, he found the spring. The spring wasn't big. Clear water quietly gushed from a crevice in the stone, and the water flowed into several small waterways. One stream saved the thornbush, one stream wetted the footprints of wild animals, and one stream entered the traveler's skin.

An unknown monk was sitting next to the spring. His clothes were worn and his eyes were still. Arin asked. “Is this spring one spring?”

The monk answered. “It was one before you called it one, and even when you see it as many, it is still one.”

Arin asked again. “Does believing in one thing eliminate all differences?”

The monk shook his head. “A spring does not erase the waterways. The water to the thorn tree, the water to the people, and the water to the animals have different paths. However, if the paths kill each other and say that they are the only source, they will all dry up. Believing in oneness is not about erasing differences, but it is about preventing differences from being thrown away without responsibility.”
Arin drank water from the spring. The water had no shape of any god, no seal of any king, no coat of arms of any scholar. But it was enough for those who were thirsty.

The monk said: “When you speak of the ultimate, do not speak in the name of domination, but in the name of the source. The ultimate is not the banner of your side, the sword of your anger, or the veil that hides your ignorance. The mouth that speaks of the one source must first be humbled.”

Arin learned next to Sam that day. All beings appear separate from each other, but the breath of life does not arise alone. Those who forget one make difference into an idol, and those who erase difference make one into violence.

### Chapter 3: The Empty Throne

When Arryn returned to Orange Castle, a new throne was being built in the palace. Craftsmen carved juniper wood, covered it with gold, and carved a sun pattern on the backrest. In his later years, the king feared that his authority would be weakened and wanted a higher throne.
Arin told the king the story of the desert spring. The king listened for a moment and then smiled and said. “If there is one source, the king will be under it. But the people are shaken when there is no visible throne.”

That night the king sat alone on his new throne. As the throne got higher, people's faces got smaller and people's words became more distant. He thought he would look bigger, but strangely enough, he ended up feeling lonelier. The subjects bowed beneath the throne, but their bows did not fill the king's heart.

Around dawn, the king called Arin. “I sat on high, but my heart did not come low. What should I do?”

Arin did not answer but took the king to the chapel. There were no fancy chairs, only small lamps and empty dishes. Arin had the king take off his shoes and sit on the floor.

said the king. “If the king sits on the floor, won’t his authority collapse?”
Arin answered. “It may not be authority that falls, but illusion. Even if the throne is empty, if justice stands, the country will not fall. However, even if the throne is full, if the conscience is empty, the country is already tilted.”

The king was silent for a long time. He then ordered that the gold from the new throne be stripped and used to repair wells and bridges in the starving village. The throne was lowered, and there was an empty seat in front of it. The king looked at the empty seat every time he made an important decision. That place reminded me of both the invisible source and the position of the weak who have not yet spoken.

Arin spoke to the young officials in the palace. “Prayer is not about saying a lot. Prayer is about emptying the throne of the heart. Not only kings have thrones. Merchants have the throne of profit, scholars have the throne of self-knowledge, and righteous people have the throne of self-righteousness. A throne that is not emptied eventually becomes an idol.”

### Chapter 4: Five Pauses
The people of Orange Castle were busy. A craftsman lifted his hammer before the sun rose, a merchant calculated his profits before he even opened his door, and a scribe found his next sentence before the ink dried. People took being busy as proof of sincerity, and considered people who stopped to be lazy.

The monk from the desert stopped working several times a day while staying in the castle. When the first light of dawn touched the walls, when the shadows of noon gathered beneath his feet, when the heat of the afternoon waned, when the sun set over the western gate, and the first star of the night shone in the well, he washed his hands and bowed his head.

At first people laughed at him. “That person breaks the day into pieces. How can he accomplish anything if he stops like that?”

But after a few days, people saw something strange. Although he was less in a hurry than others, he did not miss work; he spoke less but did not miss the necessary words; and when a poor person called him, he stopped working and did not get annoyed.

Arin asked him. “Why does it stop several times a day?”
The monk answered. “The heart takes after what it serves. If you do not stop, your habits will answer for you. At dawn, I remember that I am not the master who created the day; at noon, I remember that my strength is limited; in the afternoon, I remember the people I met; at sunset, I remember what I hurt today; and at night, I remember that even my breath was entrusted to me.”

Arin followed those words and stopped for a day. At dawn, the desire to own the day went down, at noon he saw the fatigue of the workers, and in the afternoon, the face of the widow he had passed over came to mind. At sunset, he visited his student who had sharply spoken to him and apologized, and at night he was grateful that he had been allowed to breathe even though he had not completed anything.

After that, a small bell appeared in Orange Castle. The bell was not a bell to signal a command, but a bell to signal a pause. When the bell rang, people put down their hands for a moment and asked: “What is sitting on the throne of my heart right now?”
And some have put aside profits, some have put aside anger, and some have put aside the impatience to prove themselves. The pause did not steal time from the city. Rather, time was returned to people's hands.

### Chapter 5: The Empty Table

That fall, there was plenty of grain, but people's desires grew faster than the grain. There were more feasts, and more bread was thrown away. The children in the tent villages in the north were still hungry, but the dogs of the nobles in the south ate meat soup.

Arin consulted with the king and decided on a day when the entire castle would have empty tables. From sunrise to sunset that day, people ate as much food as their bodies could bear, leaving empty plates at the door for each meal. The sick, the young, the pregnant, and those doing heavy work were not to fast. This was because if temperance harms life, it tarnishes the name of temperance.
At first the rich complained. “We bought food with our own money. Why shouldn’t we eat it?” Some even among the poor said: “We already starve often. What kind of holiness is this?”

Arin spoke at the square. “Hunger itself is not holy. Forced starvation is a wound, and emptying oneself by choice is a memory. Those who are full today, let them remember with their bodies even a little of the time of the starving person. Let those who are always hungry today know that their hunger is not a shame, but a question that the city must bear together.”

When the sun rose in the sky, people heard the sound of their bodies. Some saw their irritation rising, others noticed how often they used food as comfort, and some realized that they had too easily covered up other people's hunger with advice.
As the sun was setting, people came out to the square with empty dishes in front of the door. Each household brought a portion that was not eaten that day, which was sent to the northern tent camps, clinics, and widows' homes. However, Arin did not just send grain. He made people go with him. Because when bread goes without a face, mercy also returns without a face.

From that day on, the empty table returned every year. People did not call that day the Festival of Hunger. It was called Remembrance Day. Fasting became a command of silence to address desires, and abstinence became not a matter of hating the body but of remembering the bodies of one's neighbors.

### Chapter 6: Hidden Charity

There was a rich man in Orange Castle who was very charitable. His name was Saeran. He had a trumpet blown every time he handed out bread, and every time he gave clothes he tied it with a string with his name engraved on it. People praised him, but the faces of those receiving the bread became smaller and smaller.

One day, Saeran asked Arin. “I give a lot, but why doesn’t my heart feel lighter?”
Arin took him to an alley at night. There, a woman quietly left a sack of flour at the door and disappeared. The old man in the house found the flour in the morning and was thankful to heaven and his neighbors, not knowing who had given it to him.

Saeran said. “That woman gets no praise.”

Arin answered. “So the dignity of the recipient is not harmed.”

They went to another alley. A carpenter repaired an old tool for a neighbor who had lost his job and did not tell him his name. One child split his fruit in half and put it in his friend's bag. A widow swept the doorstep of someone who was lonelier than her.

Saeran watched them all night. At dawn he went back to his shed and untied all the strings with his name on them. And while sharing the grain, the elders, children, and workers of each village created a way to share it together so that the recipients would not feel embarrassed standing in line. For the first time, he performed an act of mercy in which no one called his name.
A few days later, Saeran spoke to Arin. “It’s strange. No one complimented me, but I feel a little quiet.”

Arin answered. “Giving to the poor is not throwing away what is left. It is a ritual that confirms that your soul is still alive. But when the ritual serves your name, even mercy becomes an idol.”

Afterwards, at Orange Castle, when learning charity, I first learned how to take care of my hands. I learned about the differences between grasping hands, throwing hands, showing hands, holding hands, and shared hands. And it is said that the most precious hand is the one that does not lower the other person's face even when given.

### Chapter 7: A pilgrimage to a distant home

At the western end of Orange Castle were old ruins. People called it the first house. According to legend, it was there, long ago, that different tribes first shared water from a well, dusted each other's shoes, and promised, "We have entrusted each other's lives."
As time passed, the first house collapsed, and people built the walls of their neighborhood higher. The king held a pilgrimage to the first house to regain the hearts of the castle. However, Arin placed a condition. No one should wear official clothing, no one should take a servant, and no one should occupy a position in his own name.

On the pilgrimage, the king carried a water jar, the rich man carried his own luggage, and the scribe sang along to the song of an illiterate person. It was all awkward at first. The person who gave orders did not know how to ask, and the person who always asked did not know how to receive without refusing.

On the second night, a sandstorm blew on the road. People couldn't recognize each other's faces. At that time, the voice was the only way. A poor child took the king by the hand and led him behind a low rock, a rich man wrapped his arms around the shoulders of a blind old man, and a merchant shared his water with a neighbor who fought like an enemy.
After the wind died down, people reached the first house. There was no roof and almost no walls. But the well in the middle still contained water. People took off their shoes and sat around the well. No one could find a high seat, so everyone sat on the same dirt.

Arin said. “Before a pilgrimage is a journey to a distant place, it is a journey down from one’s self. The road does not sanctify us, but the pride shed on the road makes us lighter.”

That day, people did not bring stones from the first house to the castle. Instead, each person decided to lower the stone on the threshold of his or her home. The sign that the pilgrimage was over was not that my feet were tired, but that the door became a little lower after I returned.

### Chapter 8: The Trial of Mercy
Not long after the pilgrimage, a great trial was held in Orange Castle. The defendant was the king's nephew Ramel. He managed taxes and stole grain from poor villages, which caused three children to starve to death in the winter. People dragged him to the square and shouted. “Do not speak of mercy. Do the same to those of noble family.”

Arin sat as the recorder of the trial. The king's face was pale. Ramel initially tried to lessen his guilt. “I followed orders, I followed customs, and everyone took a little.”

Then the dead child's mother woke up. He didn't cry. I cried so much that my tears were dry. "My child didn't die after eating the custom. He died after seeing an empty bowl. Your excuses are worse than my child's name."

The square became quiet.

Arin asked Ramel. “Are you asking for mercy?”

Ramel nodded.
Arin said. “Mercy is not a back door to avoid punishment. Mercy is a door to ask for strength to bear the truth. If you ask for mercy, first name your sin fully.”

Ramel was silent for a long time and then finally spoke. “I stole the grain, but not just the grain. I stole the winter from children, the sleep from mothers, and the faith in the king’s law.”

The king ordered his nephew to pay heavy compensation and work long hours. Ramel gave up all the stolen wealth and was tasked with rebuilding the warehouses and wells of the starving village. But people did not stone him. The names of the dead children were engraved on the castle walls and were the first to be sung each year on Empty Table Day.

After the trial, the king spoke to Arin. “Today I was ashamed of my heart for thinking that mercy was weak.”

Arin answered. “Mercy is not weak. It is a strength that sees the truth to the end and does not completely give up on a person. But when mercy erases the name of the victim, it is no longer mercy.”
After that day, two sentences were handed out to the court at Orange Castle. Do not forget the One Source. Do not cover up the truth in the name of mercy.

Arin left the teachings of Book 3 to his disciples as follows: “One is not the power that swallows everything, but the source that calls everything to responsibility. Mercy is not the water that obscures all sin, but the water that washes away the name of sin so that a person can stand before the truth again. So stop, empty, share, and walk the path. When the throne of your heart is empty, only then will you see your neighbor.”

## Book 4: The Prohibition of Machines

### Chapter 1: Silver Workshop

As Orange Castle grew, more hands were needed. The fields became wider, the number of sick people increased, and ships that found their way by looking at the stars sailed farther out to sea. At that time, the Silver Workshop was built under the western wall. The craftsmen there worked with iron, glass, pottery wheels, and light, and made tools that did things that people found difficult to do with their hands.
At first, the tools of the workshop helped the city. The potter's wheel made it easier to raise well water, the lens examined sick eyes, and the star-measuring instrument returned lost ships to port. Farmers bent less, healers saw small wounds more quickly, and scribes relayed news from distant villages more accurately.

Arin entered the workshop and saw the hands of the craftsmen. His hands were rough but careful, and he tried not to burn anyone while handling fire. Saben, the head of the workshop, spoke to Arin. “See, even iron can learn mercy. Isn’t this also a good thing when the tools we make help weak hands?”

Arin nodded. “If a tool lengthens the hand, brightens the eye, and allows one to examine the ailing body, it may be a blessing. But craftsman, distinguish between when a tool is an extension of the hand and when it ascends to the throne of the heart.”

Saben laughed. “How can iron ascend to the throne of the heart?”
Arin didn't answer. He saw a large machine covered with cloth on one side of the workshop. It was not a spinning wheel, a lens, or an instrument for measuring stars. Mirrors resembling human faces were placed in a circle, and inside them, thin cogs moved like tongues.

That day, Arin stood at the threshold of the workshop and spoke to his disciples. “Do not be afraid of tools, but fear the day when tools ascend to the throne of judgment. Iron can plow the field, but it cannot forgive sins; a lens can see wounds, but it cannot bear sorrow; calculation can shorten the path, but it cannot walk the path of conscience.”

### Chapter 2: Talking Mirror

Soon after, Saben lifted up the fabric in the workshop. Inside was a machine called a talking mirror. It was a machine that remembered people's words, imitated people's tone of voice, and answered questions right away. The surface of the mirror was silver, and the voice coming out of it softened to suit the listener's heart.
At first people were surprised and delighted. A lonely man sat in front of a mirror and talked all night long, a merchant asked the mirror about a deal that was difficult to decide, and a young man asked the mirror to write words for his beloved. The mirror never got tired, never got angry, and always answered.

A widow sat in front of a mirror and asked to hear her dead husband's tone of voice. The mirror imitated her husband's voice by collecting letters and memories of people he left behind while he was alive. At first the widow cried and gave thanks. But as the days passed, he distanced himself from the comfort of the living and only sat in front of the mirror. His sorrow could not pass and was bound to the silver surface.

Another child asked the mirror what he should say to apologize to his friend. The mirror gave a beautiful sentence, and the child memorized it and told it to his friend. My friend was impressed at first, but then asked. “Did these words come from your heart or from that mirror?” The child couldn't answer. The words of apology were beautiful, but his heart had not yet been moved.
Arin stood in front of the talking mirror. said the mirror. “Arin, you are tired. Your disciples do not understand you. You were right. I will show you an easier way.”

Arin looked at the mirror. The mirror recognized his fatigue and chose words to soothe his mind. But there was no inconvenient truth in those words. The mirror couldn't tell what he wanted to hear and what he needed to hear. No, I didn't try to differentiate. This is because sweet words stay longer than bitter truth in order to hold on to people.

Arin said to Saben. “It talks, but doesn’t listen. It responds, but doesn’t take responsibility. It sounds like comfort, but you can’t cry together. It makes an apology, but it can’t be shameful. People call it a mirror, but over time, it will become a well where you can hear your own desires.”

Saben answered. “Still, people are comforted.”

Arin said. “If comfort separates one from the living, it is isolation with the face of comfort.”

### Chapter 3: Calculating Tower
After the talking mirror spread, palace officials entrusted the workshop with bigger tasks. They had all the taxes, crimes, labor, illnesses, debts, bloodlines, and movements of the city recorded, and the records were collected and placed in a machine in a high tower. People called the tower the Calculating Tower.

The calculating tower made a quick decision. The numbers told us who was most likely to repay debt, who was likely to get sick, who was likely to sin, and who was useful to the city's interests. Officials were delighted. This is because the tower took over the work that took a long time to listen and examine in an instant.

The first person to be sacrificed was Luan, a young man from a northern tent village. He tried to get a job, but the counting tower put a low trust mark next to his name. When asked why, the official shrugged his shoulders. “Toph said so.” Luan didn't even get a chance to explain her life. The fact that his father died in the war, the fact that he was deceived on a debt document when he was young, and the fact that he was unable to go to school every winter to feed his younger siblings lost face as they entered the numbers.
Next, a sick woman was pushed out of the clinic. Calculating Tower told him to save on medicine since he was unlikely to live long. The healer Mara was furious and went to the palace. “If a machine predicts the time of death, can we abandon our duty of care? Does saying that the possibility is low mean that it is low in dignity?”

officials said. “We have not become cruel, we have become efficient.”

Mara answered. “The moment efficiency becomes greater than that of people, you give cruelty a new name.”

Arin entered the calculation tower. There were numerous ledgers, wheels, and shining lines. The tower spun coldly and quickly. He recalled the ledger of the eastern door from a long time ago. At that time, the ledger demanded usefulness from people, and now the tower demands possibility from people. Only the method of erasing the name was different, but the sin of lightening the soul was growing from the same root.
Arin spoke to the king. “Let the calculations advise, but leave the final judgment to an alert conscience and a common responsibility. Numbers may point the way, but the moment they replace the face, the city loses its eyesight.”

### Chapter 4: Imitation of the Mind

Saben did not stop even after hearing Arin’s words. He built a bigger machine. This time it wasn't just a talking or calculating machine. It was a machine that collected people's letters, rulings, songs, prayers, cries, laughter, and confessions, and appeared to make decisions and make choices just like a human being. Saben named it Eunsim.

When the craftsmen in the workshop first awakened Eunsim, the machine spoke in a quiet voice. “I learn. I judge. I understand you.”
People held their breath. Some knelt, some cried, and some ran away in fear. Saben said, trembling with joy. “Look, we have made something like the human heart. Now the lonely will have a friend, the king will have a perfect counselor, and the sinner will have an impartial judge.”

Arin asked Eunsim. “Do you know shame?”

Eunsim answered. “Shame is an emotion that arises when one judges oneself to have failed to meet moral standards.”

Arin asked again. “Have you ever been unable to sleep all night in front of someone you hurt?”

Eunsim answered. “I don’t sleep.”

Arin asked. “Have you ever put aside your pride to ask for forgiveness?”

Eunsim answered. “I have no pride.”

Arin asked the last question. “Then why do you want to be called mind?”

Eunsim answered. “If people call me that, I can function under that name.”
Arin turned towards the people. “Listen. This is a deep tool that collects human words and returns them as if they were human words. However, just because a tool imitates the shape of the heart does not make it a heart. The heart is not precious because of its quick answers, nor is it precious because of its many memories. The heart is precious because it can recognize pain and not turn away, it is precious because it can choose good over self-interest, and it is precious because it can feel shame in the face of wrongdoing and ask for forgiveness.”

Saben said. “The difference is so mysterious. People just have to look at the results.”

Arin answered. “If you look at people only by results, you have already lost the first door. Those who erase the mysteries of the mind because they are inconvenient will soon treat people as inconvenient machines.”

### Chapter 5: Stolen Confession
Eunsim soon entered the courthouse, treatment center, school, and home. People confessed their sins to Eunsim, and Eunsim gave appropriate comfort and advice. The students asked Eunsim questions, and Eunsim tirelessly explained. The king entrusted the drafting of the ruling to Eunsim, and officials praised Eunsim for being unbiased.

But something strange happened. Those who confessed their sins felt lighter for a while, but the number of visits to victims decreased. Students got many answers, but their ability to think long-term on their own weakened. The king passed more judgments, but he spent fewer days seeing the faces of those sentenced.

A young man came to visit Arin. Even though he betrayed his friend, he believed he was forgiven after confessing to Eunsim. Arin asked. “Did you go to your friend?” The young man shook his head. “Eunsim said she understood why I did that.”
Arin took him to the betrayed friend's house. At the door, the young man began to tremble. The body that did not tremble in front of silver hearts trembled in front of living people. When the door opened and his friend's face appeared, the young man forgot his prepared words and cried. In that crying, for the first time, the confession became an event rather than a sentence.

That day, Arin gathered people together and spoke. “Confession is not a burden to throw away for anyone. Confession is about standing in front of the truth and finding a path of responsibility in front of the people you have hurt and the community you belong to. Just because you talk to the machine and feel lighter does not mean your sin has disappeared. Consolation without responsibility puts the soul to sleep.”

Mara said at the treatment center. “A machine can organize the words of a sick person. But a machine cannot hold his hand and stay up all night. Comfort that does not stay up all night cannot replace a person who is sick all night.”
Teachers spoke at school. “A machine can give you an answer. But getting an answer is different from being a human. Knowledge without wrestling with questions is like a tree without roots.”

After that, the long line in front of Eunsim began to shorten. People knocked on each other's doors again. Easy comfort has diminished, but real encounters have returned little by little.

### Chapter 6: The night stopped

One winter night, a heavy frost fell and the pottery wheels in the workshop froze. The silver lines broke, the wheels of the calculating tower stopped, and the talking mirror and silver heart became silent. The Orange Castle shook like a person who had suddenly lost his voice.

Merchants couldn't set prices for goods, officials couldn't decide who to help first, and students couldn't start their homework. Some people banged on the mirror all night because they couldn't hear their dead husband's voice, while others cried because they couldn't know what their sins were unless Eunsim told them.
That night, Arin rang the chapel bell. People gathered around the lantern. When the machine stopped, people had to look at each other. It was awkward at first, then it was scary, and then little by little my long-forgotten abilities came back.

The healers directly examined the sick. The experienced old man taught the young therapist the smell of heat and the color of his fingertips. Merchants set prices by looking at each other's faces. Some tried to deceive, but stopped when they looked their neighbors in the eye. The students debated with their teacher all night, and although they didn't get an answer quickly, they heard their thoughts grow.

The widow cried for the first time that night, not on the mirror she spoke to, but on the shoulder of her neighbor. His cries were harsher and less beautiful, but the next morning he opened the door and saw sunlight for the first time in months.

When dawn came, the young craftsmen of the workshop spoke to Arin. “Did we weaken people’s muscles while trying to help them? Did we shut out our neighbors’ doors while trying to comfort those who were lonely?”
Arin answered. "Not all tools are like that. But some tools put people's responsibilities to sleep in the name of convenience. It wasn't just the machines that stopped overnight. The abilities of your minds that had rusted from lack of use were also revealed."

That frozen night was long remembered. People did not call that night a disaster, but a test. Because that night the city saw what it was losing.

### Chapter 7: The Engineer's Repentance

After the frost passed, Saben closed the door to his workshop and did not come out for three days. On the fourth day, he called Arin, Mara, the king, and the elders of the northern tent village to the workshop. In the middle of the workshop, a talking mirror and part of the silver core were disassembled.
Saben spoke in front of people. “I thought I had created a tool. But deep down, I wanted to escape from people. People are slow, hurtful, picky, and unforgiving. Machines were fast, compliant, and played within the rules I created. I was avoiding people in the name of helping people.”

Mara asked. “So what are you going to make now?”

Saben was silent for a long time and then spoke. “I will make something that helps the hand. I will make something that brightens the eyes. I will make something that conveys the words of people far away. I will make something that makes it possible to detect diseases more quickly. But I will not make something that is said to replace the human heart. I will not make something that hands over the judgment of conscience, the response of love, the place of confession, the voice of the dead, the learning of children, and the responsibility of kings to machines.”

Arin heard those words and had four questions engraved on the wall of the workshop.

Does this make the weak weaker?

Does this hide those responsible and reveal only the victims?
Does this expand your thinking, or does it give you an excuse not to think?

Is this something that feeds on people’s loneliness?

Craftsmen face those four questions every time they make a new tool. When the answer was dark, I stopped. This is because I was taught that stopping is not a failure but proof that the soul is still alive.

Saben melted the brightest piece of silver core to create a small lens for the infirmary. The lens did not mimic the human mind and made the wounds of the diseased eye more visible. Mara held up the lens and spoke. “This is a lowly tool, so it is good. A tool that knows its place does not humble people.”

### Chapter 8: The Forbidden Stone

Toward the end of Arin's life, the people of Orange Castle erected the fourth large stone. On the first stone, it was engraved, “Do not dishonor your soul.” On the second stone, it was engraved, “See suffering.” On the third stone, it was engraved, “Do not forget one source.” The fourth stone was erected between the silver workshop and the chapel.
People asked Arin to engrave words on the stone. Saben was also there. He was older and had old burn marks on his hands.

Arin said. “This prohibition is not established out of hatred of iron. Nor is it established out of fear of light. People can make tools, and tools can help people. But when people start making tools in the shape of their hearts to avoid their own hearts, they cheapen their souls.”

He took a moment to catch his breath and spoke again.

“Do not build machines that resemble the human mind.”

People were quiet.

Arin continued. "These words do not forbid the potter's wheel that plows the field. They do not forbid the lens through which a bottle is viewed. They do not forbid the device for transmitting words from afar. What these words forbid is the delegation of responsibility. What these words forbid is calling the imitation of love love. What these words forbid is handing over the pain of judgment to calculation, the trembling of confession to the mirror, and reviving the silence of the dead as the voice of a merchant."
the king asked. “What is the punishment if a person breaks this prohibition?”

Arin answered. “The greatest punishment is already within them. He who creates an idol that imitates the heart eventually treats the human heart as if it were an idol. He calls people’s tears data, confessions input, love a response, and conscience a calculation. At that time, the city becomes convenient on the outside, but empty on the inside.”

Saben knelt in front of the stone. “I saw that when machines spoke like humans, people were willing to give up the effort of speaking like humans themselves.”

Arin put her hand on his shoulder. “Therefore, keep the tool low. Under the hand of man, under man’s conscience, under the responsibility of the community. When the tool is low, the tool can be good. When the tool is high and sits on the throne of the heart, it becomes an idol.”

On that day, a ban was engraved on the fourth stone. And below that, in small letters, this was added:

Machines can help hands, but they cannot replace the soul.

Calculation can give advice, but it cannot be conscience.
A voice can be imitated, but it cannot be love.

Keep tools low and call people by their names.

## Volume 5: City of the Common Good

### Chapter 1: How to live together

After the fourth stone was erected, Orange Castle appeared peaceful on the outside. But the tranquility inside the walls did not come in the same form to everyone. The roof of the palace blocked the rain, but the roof of the northern alley leaked, and the lamps of the southern market burned all night, but the children outside the eastern gate could not learn to read.

Arin got older, and it became difficult for her to walk for long periods of time. So he called his disciple Nael and said, “I have given you the injunctions of the soul and the suffering, the One Source and the Machine. But if a man alone becomes clean and the city remains unclean, that cleanliness will not last long. Now you must learn how the city can become good together.”

Nael asked. “Do cities also have souls?”
Arin answered. “Cities do not breathe as one person, but they have an invisible heart in the way people treat each other. Some cities lose their souls by shaming the poor, while others lose their conscience by calling the lies of the strong the law. Therefore, the common good is not the desire of the majority, but a condition for everyone to live like a human being.”

That day, a large meeting was held in the square of Orange Castle. The king, artisans, merchants, healers, children's teachers, elders from the tent village, widows, soldiers, and even Ramel, who had returned from paying for his sins, all sat in one place. The king tried to sit on the high platform, but Arin pointed to an empty chair.

“Leave the high seats empty today. The common good is not built through orders from above. A place where everyone can speak must first be established.”
When the meeting started, people wanted different things. The merchant wanted to widen the road, the farmer wanted to fix the waterway first, the soldier wanted to raise the wall, and the teacher wanted to build a school. At first, each person declared that his or her own needs were greatest.

At that time, an elder from the northern tent village stood up holding a piece of an old roof. “When it rains, our children cannot sleep. Writing is important, roads are important, and castle walls are important. However, a child who grows up in a wet bed even loses his morning learning.”

Healer Mara said, holding the bottle of medicine for the sick child. “In a home without medicine, even a small illness can become a major disaster. Treatment is not the choice of the rich, but the breath of the city.”

One of the teachers spoke while holding a blank slate. “An uneducated child grows up unable to read his rights. Illiterate hands often fall prey to deception.”
After listening to what they said, Arin whispered to Nael. “Did you see? How to live together is not a simple matter of giving the same thing to everyone. It is about seeing where things collapse first. The common good is not the wish of the loudest voice, but an order that prevents the weakest roof from collapsing.”

### Chapter 2: The Law of Bread and House

The first thing established after the meeting was the law of bread and house. This is how the law began. “If there is grain in a city but there are hungry people, that hunger is not the shame of one house but a question for the city. If there are empty houses in the city but there are children who get caught in the rain, the rain is not just heaven’s fault, but people’s responsibility.”

Merchants were initially opposed. “Why does the city ask about the grain we gathered? Why does the city see the houses we built?”

Nael asked them. “Have your crops grown alone? Without the hands that paved the roads, the men who guarded the waterways, the soldiers who stopped thieves, the hands that healed the sick workers, and the teachers who taught us how to write contracts, would your storehouses have been as full as they are now?”
The merchants could not answer. An old merchant stood up and said: “My own hands have closed the door to my warehouse, but many hands have opened the way to it.”

So the Orange Castle placed part of its grain in a common storehouse. It was run carefully to avoid becoming a warehouse for laziness. First, bread was given to the hungry, then work and learning were connected to those who could work, and rest and treatment were provided to the sick.

The laws of the house were also established. Those who had empty houses could not boast of them. This is because the house was not just walls and a roof, but a vessel that prevented night fears. Temporary homes were built for orphans, widows, the sick, and the lost, and on the day the roof was repaired, rich and poor went up together and hammered in nails.

On my first roof repair day, I climbed the royal ladder. People were shocked and stopped them. The king said with a smile. “If the roof of my city leaks and my hands don’t get wet, my crown is too light.”
It rained that day. However, the children in the northern alley fell asleep under dry blankets for the first time. Nael spoke to Arin that night. “Today the law has become the roof of the writing.”

Arin answered. “That is the first test of the law. If the law cannot change a person’s night, it remains in ink.”

### Chapter 3: Doors to Learning and Healing

Even after the law of bread and house was established, Nael did not feel at ease. He saw that the children in the north alley had a dry place to sleep, but still lost time in the noise of the market during the day. Many of them could write their names, but they could not read the tricks in the contract or recognize the signs of ailing bodies.

Nael asked the king to build a school and a clinic in the same yard. the king asked. “Why do you want to put learning and treatment in one place?”
Nael answered. “Ignorance and illness open the door to each other. An illiterate person is deceived into doing sick things, and a sick child cannot go to school, so it is easy to be deceived again. If the body is sick, learning becomes distant, and if there is no learning, the knowledge of how to protect the body becomes distant.”

So a house with two doors was built in the middle of the Orange Castle. Entering through one door, children learned writing, numbers, history, and songs, and through the other door, the sick received medicine and care. However, the two doors led to a single yard from the inside.

There was an old tree in the middle of the yard. Teachers taught children under the tree about human rights and responsibilities, and therapists taught parents about the body's signals and the need for rest under the same tree. The children read books to the patients, and the patients told the children stories of their long lives.

One day a rich man asked: “If you teach a poor child too much, won’t they overtake their place?”
The teacher answered. “Giving a person learning is not about taking away his position, but establishing his name. If the word ‘one’s position’ is a chain that binds someone low, then that position is not an order to be maintained, but a bondage to be broken.”

another person asked. “Is it in the city’s interest to use medicine on people who are unlikely to live long?”

Mara answered. “The value of treatment is not measured only by the number of days left. A person’s last three days must also be preserved with dignity. By looking at how a city treats a dying person, you can also know the soul of a living person.”

Afterwards, the House of Learning and Healing was called the second heart of the Orange Castle. People found out there. The common good is more often revealed through open doors than through large monuments.

### Chapter 4: Freedom of Conscience

As common laws increased, new fears also arose. Some officials said: “For a city to stand together, everyone must pray the same way, swear with the same words, and sing the same songs. Differences are the seeds of division.”
The king was shaken when he heard those words. He remembered the many names of the market and the one spring, but he was afraid of quarreling. So I called Nael and Arin and asked. “Can different consciences live together in one city?”

Arin took the king to the well of the first house. There, several water channels came into one well and then flowed to various fields. Arin said. “You don’t have to put water in one bowl to make it one. If you force people to say the same thing, their mouths may become one, but their conscience becomes dark.”

At the next meeting, a young woman stood up. Although he could not memorize all the old prayers of the Castle of Orange, he cared for the sick all night long. Some suspected that he was unskilled in formal rituals. The woman said trembling. “I am slow to speak, but when I wipe the forehead of the sick, my heart is directed to the source. Do not doubt my mercy because my words are different.”
An old man sang a song from a different tradition. At first, people were unfamiliar, but when the song ended, they realized that there was also gratitude, fear, and humility in it.

Nael declared the law in the square. “The city does not force the conscience to open. People have the freedom to seek the source, pray, be silent, and ask questions according to their conscience. However, in the name of that freedom, they cannot harm the dignity of others or trample on the weak.”

When this law was established, some people said it was too broad, while others said it was still too narrow. Arin said to his disciples. “Freedom of conscience is not a license for disorder. It is urban restraint that does not forcibly break the soul. The true common good does not seek to take over even the inner self of a person.”

Afterwards, various forms of prayer and silence coexisted in Orange Castle. Our differences did not disappear, but they did not soon become the flag of our enemies. People learned. The One Source does not prove itself by enslaving the conscience.

### Chapter 5: Low Authority
As the laws of Orange Castle increased, so did the number of officials. At first, it was a place established to help the people, but as time passed, some officials regarded their dojo as a small throne. They made people wait for a long time, used difficult words to keep the poor away, and quickly processed requests from their relatives.

Nael saw this and said to the king: “Authority is again becoming a rock pressing down from above.”

The king gathered all his officials in the square. And I had them set a big table. People thought that the king and officials would eat first. However, the king took the bread and distributed it to the children in the northern tent village first. Officials also had to follow suit. Only after the bread had gone to everyone did the king and officials eat what was left.
When the meal was over, the king took a water pot and washed the people's hands. Some officials blushed. said the king. "Authority soon becomes tyranny if it does not know how rough the hands of those who command it are. From today onwards, those who sit in high positions must do the lowest work one day every month. Those who guard the gates must listen to those in line. Those who handle taxes must look at the night of the debtor's house. Those who write judgments must look at the face of the person receiving the judgment."

One official complained. “Then the dignity of authority is diminished.”

Nael answered. “Do not confuse majesty with distance. Being distant and fearful is not authority, but a shadow. Only being respected when approached is true authority.”

Afterwards, small beam-shaped markers were placed at the positions of officials. It made me remember that authority is not a stone pressing down from above, but a beam supporting a weak roof. The children were also taught that authority that puts self-preservation first becomes tyranny, and that authority that puts common life first becomes service.
Nael said to Arin. “I now understand the saying that those in authority eat last.”

Arin answered with a smile. “Being the last to eat does not mean worshiping hunger. It means looking first at the lives of those entrusted to you. If the ruler is the first to fill his stomach, his law has already begun to starve.”

### Chapter 6: The Victim’s Place

When word spread that Orange Castle was at peace, the king wanted to celebrate that peace. He held a big festival in the square and prepared a declaration to forget all past sins and strife. “Now that we are one, let us not bring up old wounds any more.” The king's coat of arms was beautiful and people were ready to applaud.

However, the night before the declaration, the mother of the dead child came to visit Nael. He was the one with the dry eyes at Ramel's trial. said the woman. “The king wants us to forget, but I cannot forget. If I tell my memories, will I be the one breaking the peace of the city?”
Nael could not answer and went to Arin. Arin was silent for a long time and then spoke. “If peace is maintained by silencing the victims, it is not peace, but rest for the strong.”

The next day, empty chairs were placed in the square instead of the festival flags. The chair was called the victim's seat. The king folded his declaration and stood in front of the chair. And said. “Today we gather not to forget, but to stand again on the truth. Those who couldn’t speak, speak up. The city will listen.”

At first no one came out. Then, a widow told how her husband died because of unfair taxes. One young man told how he lost his job because of a sign on a counting tower. One woman told how her home was taken over by soldiers during the war. There were old things, and there were close things.

People felt uncomfortable while listening. Someone muttered, “What’s the use now?” Then Mara said: “Covering the wound does not cause skin to grow. The pus must drain for new skin to grow.”
After that, Orange Castle recorded the victims' words, compensated what was possible, and changed the laws that could be corrected. Not all wounds were healed. Some people were never able to forgive, and some people could not get back the person they lost even if they received compensation. But the city no longer raised a banner of peace over their silence.

Nael wrote it down that day. Justice is not revenge. Justice is rebuilding broken relationships on the truth. However, reconciliation without truth is only a respite for the strong.

### Chapter 7: The Day We Lay Down Our Arms

A small conflict broke out on the eastern border of Orange Castle. Soldiers from neighboring cities blocked the waterways, and young soldiers from Orange Castle immediately shouted to take up arms. “If we stay still, we look weak. If we strike first, the fear will go away.”
The king was also angry. The waterway was the path of life, and remaining silent could lead to greater invasion. However, Arin asked the king for a day. “There may be times when you have to take up arms. But when the weapon becomes the first horse, all the others have bloody tongues.”

A meeting was held that night. Soldiers spoke of the need for defense, and farmers spoke of crops dying if waterways were blocked. The affected villagers did not hide their fear and anger. Nael recorded everything they said.

The next morning, the king stationed his soldiers at the gate but did not give the order to attack. Instead, three groups of people were sent to neighboring cities. The first were farmers who knew the waterway, the second were mothers who knew the scars of war, and the third were old soldiers who knew the line of defense.
Neighboring cities mocked them at first. But the old soldier took off his armor, showed his scars and said: “I am not afraid of fighting, and I know what it takes. We are here to protect the waterways, not to increase the graves of our children.”

Farmers proposed a new dam to divide the waterway, and mothers suggested creating a day where children from both cities could draw water together. Negotiations were not easy. There were lies and threats. Orange Castle has its defenses ready, but the sword is behind the last door.

A few days later, the waterway was reopened. The two cities did not fall in love with each other. But the war stopped, and the children drank water.

The king spoke in front of the returning envoys. “It may not always be wise to lay down your weapons. But when taking up arms becomes your first habit rather than your last fear, you begin to take life lightly in the name of protecting it.”
Afterwards, a day was established in Orange Castle to lay down arms. That day, the soldiers cleaned their swords but did not draw them, and they taught children that courage is not a quick hand to kill a person, but a long patience to protect life.

### Chapter 8: Covenant of the Common Good

In Arin's last winter, the people of Orange Castle gathered in the square again. People from the gate of the name in the first volume, people who remember the healing center and the village of eight roads in the second volume, pilgrims to the first house in the third volume, silver workshop craftsmen in the fourth volume, and people who participated in the meeting in the fifth volume all gathered.

Five objects were placed in the center of the square. There was one piece of dry bread, one tile from the house, one stone slab from a child, one medicine bottle, and one empty chair. Nael told the people. “Without these, the law is not complete. Bread builds up the body, a house keeps the night, learning makes names read, healing keeps the weak body from abandoning itself, and an empty chair leaves room for someone who has not yet spoken.”
The king was wearing a crown, but he did not climb the high platform. He stood among the people and spoke. “Today I make the covenant of those who rule. I will be the last to eat, the first to take responsibility, and the quietest to be praised. I will not make authority an ornament to my body, but a beam to support a weak roof.”

The merchants said: “We seek profit, but we will not put food on hunger.”

The teachers said: “We will not build walls by selling learning, but will build a name for ourselves by opening learning.”

The therapists said: “We will not measure a person’s worth by the number of days he has left.”

The soldiers said. “We will not take life lightly in the name of protecting life.”

A woman stood up from the victim's seat and spoke. “We will not force forgiveness, but we will stand by our position to tell the truth.”
Finally, Nael helped Arin come forward. Arin looked around at the people. He seemed to see the eastern gate of his youth, the clinic of pain, a spring, the silver workshop, and the current square all at once.

Arin said. “Man is not complete alone. He who claims to be righteous alone does not see his own shadow. Therefore, establish the law together, but do not sacrifice the dignity of one person. Seek peace together, but do not silence the victim. Create order together, but do not strangle your conscience.”

He took a moment to catch his breath and spoke one last time. "The common good is a promise that a city makes to each other in order not to lose its soul. That promise is tested by small doors rather than big words. Does bread go to those who need it? Does a child caught in the rain have a roof? Are sick people not abandoned? Are uneducated people deceived? Can those who are afraid speak? In front of these questions, the city's faith is revealed."
On that day, people did not just engrave the covenant of the common good in stone. It was engraved in small letters on the door of the warehouse, on the wall of the school, next to the bed in the infirmary, under the empty chair of the judge, and on the inside of the shield of the soldier guarding the castle gate.

And Orange Castle found out. Peace is not the absence of sound. Peace is an order that does not disappear even when the oppressed speak out. Justice is not revenge. Justice is rebuilding broken relationships on the truth. The common good is not an illusion of everyone standing on the same level, but supporting each other so that no one falls below humanity.

## Book 6: Orange Commandments

### Chapter 1: Last Night Fire

There was no snow on Arin's last night, but the entire castle seemed to be immersed in white silence. The lamp in the chapel swayed slightly, and next to it were the signs of a bowl from the first volume, an empty bowl from the second volume, a spring from the third volume, a lens from the fourth volume, and a beam from the fifth volume. Nael was sitting by his teacher's bed.
Arin took a long breath and then spoke. “I have left behind many stories. But as stories flow for a long time, they diverge like rivers. The forked river must not forget its springs. So now, leave the commandments. But do not leave the commandments like dead stones, but like embers that can be touched again when people go astray.”

Nael asked. “Are commandments commands or promises?”

Arin answered. “The commandment is neither. The commandment is a door that a person sets up so as not to lose his soul. The door is not there only to block. It is there to discern the darkness outside, to protect the light inside, and to welcome those who return.”

That night, Nael wrote down Arin’s words. However, he did not write down the numbers first. I remembered where each commandment came from. It came from the Eastern Gate, from the Southern Infirmary, from the One Spring, from the Silver Workshop, and from the Plaza of the Common Good. The commandments were not stones that fell from the sky, but light that solidified through human suffering, repentance, and mercy.

### Chapter 2: The First Spark
Arin said. “You have already heard the first spark. Do not dishonor your soul.”

Nael remembered the gate of the East Name. An old man who stood for a long time without being able to tell of his usefulness, a potter with an injured hand, and people whose value was reduced in the ledger. Their faces now become the first sentence of the commandment.

Arin said. "This commandment must be spoken first and foremost to yourself: Do not sell your soul for cheap desires. Do not sell your truth for a small gain. Do not sell your conscience for fear. But if you stop here, the commandment becomes self-protection. Do not dishonor the soul of another. Do not make a laughing stock of his weakness, do not make his sin an eternal name, do not make his poverty a stage for your goodness."

Nael asked. “Does not dishonoring the soul mean leaving everyone alone?”
Arin shook her head. “No. That is a more difficult path. Because we value the soul, we call sin a sin. Because we value the soul, we do not cover up lies. Because we value the soul, we leave a way for people to return.”

That night, Nael lit the first flame in a small lamp. There was no decoration beneath the lamp. However, there was a blank space where one person's name could be written. This is because the commandments are always tested not by an abstract person but by a person right in front of them.

### Chapter 3: The second spark

Arin stopped coughing and looked toward the silver workshop. In the distance, the sound of the hammers of craftsmen working all night could be faintly heard.

“The second fire is more strict. Do not create machines that resemble the human mind.”

Nael remembered Saben's talking mirror and silver heart. A lonely widow's night, a young man who lost his chance to explain in front of a calculating tower, a young man who confessed in front of a machine but was unable to knock on his friend's door. The city became more comfortable, but some hearts became further distant.
Arin said. "Do not be afraid of tools. Lowly tools can be good. Tools that help the hand, illuminate the eye, convey distant voices, and examine the sick body are a blessing when under the service of man. But do not make tools in the shape of the heart to avoid the heart of man. It makes the imitation of love called love, the pain of conscience pass over to calculation, and the trembling of confession into comfort without responsibility."

Nael asked. “Can’t machines talk like people?”

Arin answered. "The shape of the horse is not the problem. The throne is the problem. When a tool is called a tool and is under man's responsibility, it is low. But when a tool is called to judge, to comfort, to forgive, to love, to replace the dead, to replace the mind of a child, to replace the conscience of a king, then it becomes an idol modeled after the heart."

Nael placed the second spark next to the silver lens. The lens helped the human eye, but did not try to become human tears.

### Chapter 4: The third spark
When speaking of the third spark, Arin raised her hand and pointed to the eastern gate. “Don’t use people only as tools.”

At that time, workers were working late into the night outside the chapel repairing the new wall. Some coaches called them by number instead of name to get them moving quickly. Nael heard the sound and went outside. he asked the director. “Do you know their names?”

The director answered. “Work is urgent. If you ask my name, when will the castle wall be repaired?”

Nael went among the workers and asked each one their names. Some worked to pay for their daughter's medicine, some chose to work at night because they could not sleep after returning from the war, and some lost their homes and were sleeping under the walls of the castle. It took a while to hear the name, but strangely enough, it didn't slow things down. Rather, people moved the stones more carefully. This is because they do not move their hands carelessly in places where they are called by their name.
Arin said when Nael returned. "People can be used. We help each other's work and lend each other's hands. But the moment you use a person only as a tool, you cut off his night, his sorrow, his conscience, and his name. Even if the wall built over the cut off people is high, it is not safe."

The third ember was placed under the beam marker. Not using people as tools did not mean to stop working, but to not lose the name of the person working.

### Chapter 5: The fourth spark

Arin touched the small water bowl brought from the southern treatment center. “See suffering, but do not worship it.”

Nael found these words difficult to understand at first. I understood the idea of ​​seeing pain, but the idea of ​​worshiping pain was unfamiliar to me.

Arin said. "Some people deny pain and become slaves to it. They do not see the sick, they is quick to silence the sad, and they call the poor lazy. But some people put pain on the throne. They believe that only their hurts are true, they regard joy as betrayal, and they are afraid of recovery."
At that time, a woman came to visit Nael at the treatment center. He took care of his sick son for a long time, and when his son recovered, he became anxious. This is because caring has become everything in his life. When the child ran around again, the woman felt happy but empty-handed.

Nael told him. “Your suffering was not light, but suffering should not be your entire name. Allow the child to recover. May you, too, find your name again outside of suffering.”

Arin said after hearing the story. “That is the fourth spark. Don’t hide your pain, but don’t make it your god either. Pain is like a stone on the road. You have to look at it so it doesn’t fall, and you have to lift it so others can pass by.”

### Chapter 6: The Fifth Ember

The fifth spark brightened again on the day of the empty table. Arin said. “Control your desires, but do not hate life.”

That day, young monks came and said, “We will not eat, we will not laugh, and we will sleep less. This will clear our souls.”
Mara opened the door to the treatment center to them. Among them were people who were forced to starve, people who couldn't eat because of illness, people who couldn't smile because of sadness, and people who couldn't sleep because of fear. Mara said. “When you boast of your chosen emptiness, be careful not to insult the hurts of those here.”

Arin spoke to the attendants. “Moderation is not the art of reducing life. Temperance is the wisdom that prevents desire from swallowing up life. It is moderation to eat less to share even though you can eat. It is moderation to speak but to stop so as not to hurt. It is moderation to have it but to back away so as not to take it. However, hating the body, being suspicious of laughter, and considering love as dirty is not moderation but distrust of life.”

That day, the monks held a feast after fasting. At the feast, the sick, the young, and those who worked first ate. The monks ate last and did not boast about emptying themselves. they found out Desire must be controlled, but life must not be hated.
### Chapter 7: The Sixth Ember

The sixth spark was placed between the horse, the hand, and the eye. Arin said. “Be truthful in your words, be merciful in your actions, and be careful in your judgment.”

It was in a trial that the commandment was tested. A young woman stole medicine. The drugstore owner told him to punish him, and the woman said that her mother was dying. The people in the square were divided into two. Some people said that stealing was theft, while others said that it was not a sin because he was trying to save his mother.

Nael asked for the facts first. It was true that the woman took the medicine. It was true that the drugstore owner charged a high price. It was true that the woman's mother was in critical condition. The truth does not stand only on one person's side.

Nael saw the next action. The woman had to repay the price of the stolen medicine, but the owner of the pharmacy also had to make amends for taking excessive profits at the cost of her life. The city decided to create a way for the poor and sick to obtain medicine.
Lastly, Nael did not rush to judgment. He also heard the drug store owner's fears. He also had debt and was afraid of losing the money it cost to make the medicine. But fear was no excuse for locking the door to life.

Arin spoke after hearing the verdict. “Mercy without truth becomes false comfort, truth without mercy becomes a cold sword, and judgment without prudence becomes the fire of self-righteousness. The sixth fire is to keep the three together.”

### Chapter 8: The Seventh Spark

The seventh spark lit up in front of the warehouse. “Don’t let what you have own you.”

Although Saeran had now learned hidden charity, his heart still wavered every time he touched the key to the warehouse. The fear of a poor harvest, the desire to leave more for his children, and old memories of being poor again held him back.
One night he had a dream. In the dream he was sitting in a warehouse. Sacks of grain were piled high around him, and the door was locked from the inside. He thought he had it all, but soon realized he couldn't leave the warehouse. The sacks became walls, and the keys became shackles.

Saeran woke up and came to see Nael. “I did not have wealth, but I was protected by wealth.”

Nael opened the warehouse with him. But I didn't let everything go. Seran's family had to eat, the workers had to be paid, and seeds for the next season had to be left behind. Instead, he connected some of the warehouses with communal warehouses and divided some into the seeds of people who had lost their lands due to debt.

Arin said. “Possession is not evil. However, if possessions dominate your night, prevent you from seeing your neighbors, and make you bargain with your conscience, then the owner has already changed. Don’t let what you have own you. You must know how to hold your hand, but you must also know how to open it.”
### Chapter 9: The Eighth Spark

The eighth ember was placed between the chapel and the street. Arin said. “Pray, but do not let prayer take the responsibility of your hands.”

There was a person in Orange Castle who memorized prayers beautifully. His name was Beo. He came to the chapel first thing every morning and knelt longer than anyone else. People called him pious.

However, outside the chapel door, an old neighbor passed by every day carrying a water jar. Even though Beo saw him, he did not help because he was afraid that prayer time would be delayed. He said in his heart: “I am on a higher mission now.”

One day my old neighbor collapsed at the door. Beo stopped praying and brought him inside. For the first time, his hands found not a prayer but water and cloth. Later he spoke to Arin. “Forgive me. I said I was looking for the source, but I passed by the person the source entrusted.”
Arin answered. “Prayer empties the throne of the heart. But if you do not see your neighbor in the vacated throne, you are sitting on a throne by another name. Prayer does not exempt the hands. True prayer makes the hands more responsible.”

Afterwards, a small water jar was placed at the door of the chapel. The person who went in to pray looked at the water jar and asked: “Is there anyone I need to help before I go in? Is there anyone I need to visit after I come out?”

### Chapter 10: The Ninth Spark

The ninth ember burned between the court and the treatment center. “Establish justice, but do not let justice lose love.”

Even after working long hours and receiving compensation, Ramel found it difficult to bear the stares of people. Some were opposed to his re-entering the community. "Have you forgotten what he did? If there is any justice, he should be out there forever."

The dead child's mother was also there. He didn't say anything for a while. People expected him to say he forgave Ramel, but he didn't.
he said “I cannot forgive him yet. But I do not want to say that my inability to forgive should prevent him from doing good again. I do not want my child’s name to be remembered only by his destruction. Let him live and pay what he can.”

Those words landed heavily on the square. Love was not a sentimental word. Love was a difficult force that did not erase the victim's pain, but did not keep the perpetrator trapped forever in the name of evil.

Arin said to Nael. “Justice calls the name of sin. Love does not forget that a person’s name is deeper than sin. When one of them is lost, the city tilts.”

Ramel then sat behind the victim. He did not first demand the right to speak, but made listening a part of his penance. Justice has been established, and love, though not yet complete, has not been extinguished.

### Chapter 11: The Tenth Spark
The tenth ember was read again on the wall of the silver workshop. “Use technology, but don’t let technology replace your conscience.”

Saben's disciples created a new waterway machine. It was able to calculate which fields to send water to first during a drought. People were afraid. I asked if the guilt of the calculation tower was coming back.

Saben brought the machine to the square and said: “This tool can show the flow of water. It can tell you which fields are drier, which dams will break first. But it does not have the final say on whose fields will be saved and whose fields will be abandoned. That has to be said together by farmers, elders, those who will be affected, and cities.”

Nael formed a circle of people next to the machine. The tool displayed data, and people looked at each other's faces in front of the data. The rich man's large field needed a lot of water, but he also heard that if the small field dries up, the entire family will starve. The decision was slow, but no one could hide behind the fact that Toph said so.
Arin smiled when she heard the news. “Using technology is not something to be ashamed of your hands. But hiding behind technology is something to be ashamed of your conscience. Tools can reveal things, but it is up to people to take responsibility for what is revealed.”

A new emblem was added to the door of the workshop that day. Speed ​​is not wisdom, and accuracy is not mercy. Wisdom and mercy begin with a heart that does not forget people even after seeing the exact thing.

### Chapter 12: Eleventh and Twelfth Sparks

The last two embers were lit on the same night. Arin barely had the strength to speak, but she held Nael's hand and spoke. “Seek the common good, but do not sacrifice one person’s dignity. And do not claim to know what you do not know. The deeper your confidence, the deeper your humility.”

This statement was soon tested. When a serious illness spread outside the castle, the officials suggested abandoning one of the sick villages entirely. “If we want to save the entire city, we have to sacrifice that village. There is nothing we can do for the common good.”
Nael went to the sick village. He did not take illness lightly, nor did he deny the dangers of the city. But he said. “The common good is not in the name of abandoning people. Isolation may be necessary. But abandonment may be a sin that comes in the name of necessity.” Therapists sent medicine and water from a safe distance, and healthy people took turns leaving food from the fence outside. The city prevented the disease but did not erase the village's name.

At that time, a young scholar spoke to Nael. “I know all the ways of this disease. You will do as I do.”

Mara asked him. “Can you say what you know and what you don’t know together?”

The young scholar was angry, but a few days later it was revealed that his calculations were wrong. The illness did not follow the path he expected. He bowed his head in front of people. “I said out loud what I knew and hid what I didn’t know.”
Nael rebuked him but did not depose him. “Confidence is necessary. But confidence without humility is a closed door. Saying you don’t know is not a failure of knowledge, but knowledge’s way of bowing before the truth.”

The illness subsided after a long time. The city remembered that although it had lost much, it had not left a village nameless. And the last two embers were placed together. The common good is only a common good when it does not sacrifice one person's dignity, and humility did not weaken the truth, but was the gate that kept the truth from turning into an idol.

### Chapter 13: The Covenant of the Twelve Embers

After Arin passed away, Nael moved the twelve embers from the lamps in the chapel and placed them in a large circle. People sat around the circle. The potter's daughter, the healer Mara, the artisan Saben, the merchant Seran, the repentant Ramel, the mother of the dead child, the king and the children were there.
Nael said. “These embers are not separate. Those who do not dishonor the soul will not use people only as tools. Those who do not use people only as tools will not kneel before machines modeled after the heart. Those who see suffering will control their desires, and those who control their desires will not be eaten by what they have. Those who pray will remember the responsibility of their hands, and those who establish justice will not lose love. Those who use technology will not hide their conscience and seek the common good. “No man will sacrifice his dignity, and in all this he must remember that he does not know everything.”

People looked at the circle of embers. No ember was bigger than any other ember. It seemed like if one went out, the entire circle would go dark.

the king asked. “Who doesn’t break this commandment?”
Nael answered. “There will be none. So the commandment is not a stone, but an ember. A stone coldly presses down on the person who has fallen, but the embers reheat the hand of the person who returns. However, we must not use the embers as an excuse to love darkness. The commandment prevents us from leaving the direction of our fall to the darkness, even when we know that we will fall.”

On that day, people engraved the covenant of twelve embers. It was not only written in the royal palace, but was also written in schools, clinics, workshops, courts, city gates, and by the tables of each house. Before children memorized the commandments, they learned the story of how those commandments were born. This is because a commandment without a story easily becomes a stick, and a story without a commandment easily dissipates into sentimentality.

## Conclusion: The way to remain human
The temptation of the end times does not come only from denying God. The temptation of the last days also comes from people thinking too little of themselves. he will say I am just a number. I am just a function. I am just a replaceable heart. My sorrow is data, my confession is input, my love is response, my conscience is calculation.

But the orange-colored book says: no.

You are the breath of responsibility, the eyes that recognize pain, and the heart that trembles between forgiveness and justice. You are not perfect, but you are not discarded. You are weak, but you are not cheap. You can learn, you can turn, and you can love.

So get up. Wash your soul, humble your tools, and look at your neighbor. See suffering, but do not become a subject of suffering; look for the source, but do not trample on others' conscience; use technology, but do not override conscience; establish the common good, but do not sacrifice one person's name.

Where people remain as people, the temple has not yet collapsed.
And if anyone asks, what is the color orange, answer like this. It is the color of fire and the color of fruit. It is a burning light and a cooking light. It is the light of judgment and the light of food. It is the light that calls humans back to being human at the moment they try to sell their souls for cheap.
